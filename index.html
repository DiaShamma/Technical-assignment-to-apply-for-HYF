Hello, I'm Albert Einstein
Theoretical Physicist

You may have heard of me because of my groundbreaking work on the theory of relativity and the structure of atoms. In fact, I won the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1921 for my explanation of the photoelectric effect. On this page, you can learn more about my life, my theories, my accomplishments, my legacy, and my thoughts.

My Story

Born on March 14, 1879 in Ulm, Germany, I made the decision to drop out of school at a young age when my family moved to Milan, Italy in 1894. Despite not finishing high school, I was able to pass the entrance exam for the Swiss Federal Polytechnic in Zurich, Switzerland and began studying there in 1896. I excelled in my studies and graduated in 1900 with a degree in physics and mathematics. After graduation, I worked as a patent examiner in Bern, Switzerland.

1905, I made a significant impact on the scientific community when I published four groundbreaking papers in the journal "Annalen der Physik," which established me as a leading scientist in the field. These papers introduced my theory of relativity and made significant contributions to the fields of quantum theory, statistical mechanics, and electromagnetism. I received my doctorate from the University of Zurich in 1908 and went on to hold academic positions at various universities in Europe, including the University of Prague and the University of Berlin. In 1914, I accepted a position at the Prussian Academy of Sciences in Berlin and became a German citizen. However, due to the rise of the Nazi Party and my Jewish heritage, I left Germany in 1933 and immigrated to the United States, where I took a position at Princeton University. I became a naturalized American citizen in 1940 and remained at Princeton until my retirement in 1945. I passed away on April 18, 1955 in Princeton, New Jersey, leaving behind a legacy as one of the most influential and renowned scientists in history.

As a young boy, I was a curious and bright student, but I struggled to conform to the strict and regimented nature of the school system when my family moved and I switched from a Catholic elementary school to a public school. Instead of focusing on rote learning and obedience, I preferred to explore my own interests, such as reading and playing the violin.

My Theories

The theory of special relativity

I developed the theory of special relativity in 1905, which explains how the laws of physics are the same for all non-accelerating observers, regardless of their relative motion. The theory introduces the concept of space-time, a four-dimensional entity that combines space and time.

According to the theory, time passes more slowly in stronger gravitational fields, a phenomenon known as time dilation. This has been confirmed through experiments, including the Hafele-Keating experiment.

My equation E=mc^2, published in the same year, relates the energy of a particle to its mass and has had numerous applications, including in nuclear power and the behavior of black holes.

The theory of general relativity

In 1915, I developed the theory of general relativity, which explains how gravity works by describing it as the curvature of space-time caused by the presence of matter and energy. The theory predicts that time passes more slowly in stronger gravitational fields, a phenomenon known as time dilation, which has been confirmed through experiments including the Pound-Rebka experiment. The theory has had numerous successful explanations and predictions, including the orbits of planets, the bending of light around massive objects, and the expansion of the universe.

The theory of general relativity also successfully predicted the precession of the perihelion of Mercury and the gravitational redshift of light, both later confirmed through observation.

The Theory of Electromagnetism

The theory of electromagnetism explains the relationship between electricity and magnetism as part of the electromagnetic field. Before my work in 1905, electricity and magnetism were thought to be unrelated and described by separate laws. The theory states that electric and magnetic fields are different aspects of the electromagnetic field, described by Maxwell's equations published in 1865. These equations predict the behavior of light, an electromagnetic wave, and have numerous applications, including in electric motors and generators, and technologies such as radio and radar.

The theory of electromagnetism is now considered one of the fundamental theories of physics and is crucial in our understanding of the universe.

The theory of quantum mechanics

The theory of quantum mechanics explains the behavior of subatomic particles using the concept of wave-particle duality. It was developed in the early 20th century to understand the behavior of atoms and molecules that couldn't be explained by classical physics. The theory uses quantum mechanics, a mathematical formalism that predicts the probability of finding a particle in a particular location or with a particular energy, but not the exact outcome of a measurement. This fundamental uncertainty is known as the Heisenberg uncertainty principle.

The theory of quantum mechanics has had numerous applications, including in the explanation of the behavior of atoms and molecules and in the development of technologies such as transistors and lasers. It is now considered one of the fundamental theories of physics and is crucial in our understanding of the behavior of the universe at the atomic and subatomic scales.

The photoelectric effect

The photoelectric effect is a phenomenon in which electrons are emitted from a metal surface when light is shone on it. I explained this effect in a series of papers published in 1905, which was the first experimental evidence supporting the idea that light can be described as a particle, or photon. According to the theory of the photoelectric effect, when a photon with sufficient energy hits a metal surface, it can knock an electron out of the metal, transferring its energy to the electron and giving it kinetic energy. The energy of the photon is related to its frequency, with higher frequency photons having more energy, and the minimum energy required to eject an electron is known as the work function of the metal.

The photoelectric effect has had numerous applications, including in solar cells, photodetectors, and lasers, and is an important part of our understanding of the nature of light and its interaction with matter. It was recognized with the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1921.

The theory of cosmic inflation

The theory of cosmic inflation explains the rapid expansion of the universe during the first fraction of a second after the Big Bang, during which it increased in size by a factor of at least 10^78. This rapid expansion smoothed out any irregularities, leading to the uniformity of the cosmic microwave background radiation and the large-scale structure of the universe observed today. The theory was developed in the 1980s and is supported by observational evidence, including the uniformity of the cosmic microwave background radiation and the large-scale structure of the universe. It is now considered a key part of the standard model of cosmology.
